The Florida Keys Corrosion Problem: 316L vs 304 Steel

The Florida Keys present the most extreme corrosive environment for door hardware on the East Coast. The combination of salt spray, humidity, warm temperatures, and constant ocean air creates conditions that destroy standard hardware at a rate most homeowners — and most contractors — dramatically underestimate.

The Two Stainless Steels

304 stainless steel is what most door hardware is made from. It's considered "stainless" because it resists rust in normal conditions. In the Keys, it lasts 12–18 months before significant corrosion sets in.

316L stainless steel contains 2–3% molybdenum, which dramatically improves resistance to chloride corrosion (the type caused by salt water and salt air). It is the standard for marine applications — boat fittings, dock hardware, coastal architecture. In the Keys, 316L lasts 8–12 years.

Why Most Contractors Use 304

316L costs more. Most general handymen and even some door companies use standard 304 hardware because it is cheaper and "stainless steel" sounds good enough. In the Keys, it is not. You'll pay for the same repair 4–6× over a decade.
